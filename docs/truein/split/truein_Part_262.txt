What are the best practices for absence management? What is absence management? Absence management is a way to reduce absenteeism at work by effectively tracking and handling employee absences. It is part of workforce management and essential to ensure smooth operations and high productivity. While a simple process, effective absence management requires a systematic process of establishing policies, procedures, and tools to monitor and manage employee time off. Furthermore, it includes ensuring proper documentation and complying with legal requirements, and simultaneously implementing strategies for handling and tracking employee absences from work. In simplest terms, absence management makes it possible for employers to balance the workforce’s le gitimate needs for time off and operational efficiency. Importance of absence management Absence management is critical for any organization, regardless of industry and workforce type. Unplanned absence can wreak havoc on business productivity and cause revenue losses. Minimizing unplanned absences will help you improve workforce availability, boosting productivity and simplifying cost management. Apart from these tangible reasons, absence management also has some intangible significance. Unchecked absenteeism is a reason for stress and dissatisfaction among employees who have to pick up the extra work. High absenteeism indicates something is fundamentally wrong with your business; it can be too much stress, toxic culture, or insufficient training. Here are some of the reasons why absence management is essential. 1.Operational continuity With effective absence management, you can ensure enough workers are available for the scheduled shifts so business operations can continue smoothly. Absence tracking helps businesses anticipate staffing needs and redistribute workload to minimize disruptions and maintain productivity. 2. Productivity Without enough workers to take on the shifts, productivity is directly impacted. Absence management is how you can identify trends, address recurring absences, and take necessary steps to mitigate their effects on productivity. 3. Compliance with laws and regulations Another primary reason to focus on absence management is to ensure you comply with the local employment laws and regulations. The laws and regulations related to different types of leaves, such as statutory sick leave, family leave, or other mandated leaves, differ from state to state. Absence management makes it easier to adhere to legal obligations. This ensures that your business avoids potential legal issues and penalties. Here is a complete guide on sick leave policy for companies . 4. Employee engagement Absence management ensures fairness and consistency in how a company handles absences. It demonstrates your commitment to staff’ s well-being and work-life balance. By implementing clear policies, workers know the expectations and procedures for requesting and reporting absences. Transparent communication and support mechanisms make employees feel valued and foster a positive work environment, boosting employee satisfaction, engagement, and retention. Benefits of effective absence management Managing an organization is a multifaceted responsibility; absence management plays a vital role in the process. With effective attendance management, you can: 1. Improve productivity Organizations can easily minimize schedule disruptions and maintain high productivity by proactively managing absences. Proper absence management ensures that all shifts are covered, and there is even distribution of work between employees. This supports team performance and workflow. 2. Cost savings As mentioned, unplanned absences can be a significant revenue leak for a business. You can plug such revenue loss by accurately tracking and managing employee time off. It saves you from hiring temporary workers, paying overtime, and reducing productivity. 3. Workforce planning When you use attendance management systems like Truein for tracking absences, you get valuable data and insights related to workforce planning. These insights provide a close view of absence patterns and trends, indicating whether you need better policies or additional staff for better operational efficiency and workforce planning. 4. Improved health and well-being A positive work environment focuses on employee health and well-being, allowing them time off when needed. Absence management empowers managers to appropriately support workers’ physical and mental health requirements, reducing the risk of burnout. This adds to their work-life balance, and workers have high satisfaction and engagement at work. For all these benefits, you must focus on the critical components of absence management that we will discuss next. Critical components of absence management Depending on your industry and workforce structure, there can be myriad strategies for absence management, but most have these standard components: 1. Absence policies Every company must have a clear and comprehensive attendance policy that outlines everything related to leave management, including how to request time off, notice requirements, types of leaves allowed, etc. The guidelines are also mandatory to comply with the applicable employment laws and regulations related to absences and leaves. 2. Absence tracking and recording To effectively manage absenteeism , it is essential to track and record absences accurately. Attendance monitoring is another critical component that allows organizations to monitor absence trends and track leave balances for each employee. 3. Leave request There must be a standardized process for workers to request time off. Your workforce must know the process for submitting a leave application or how to use an absence management system (if in place). 4. Notification Your workers must be aware of the absence reporting procedures. It includes information about who to notify when to notify, and any required documentation when applying for a leave. 5. Absence analysis and reporting For effective absence management, data analysis, and reporting are essential. Managers can identify absenteeism trends, patterns, and potential issues by reviewing absence records. Organizational Challenges faced due to lack of absence management Suboptimal use of time and resources Using timesheets and Excel spreadsheets, are not ideal for absence tracking because they are time-consuming, error-prone due to human inaccuracy, and require frequent updates. Automated absence management tools can improve accuracy by reducing the risk of human error. This helps to ensure that the data is accurate and reliable. Legal compliance Varying local employment laws and regulations require compliance. Your absence policy should consider these compliance requirements. Time theft Time theft refers to the act of employees clocking in and out for work when they are not actually present or having someone else clock attendance for them . This can often leave workers who don’t indulge in time theft feeling frustrated, angry, and resentful. Time theft could also potentially result in decreased morale and productivity amongst impacted workers. An automated time and attendance system using modern biometrics such as face recognition helps to resolve these problems by making it more difficult for workers to falsify their time records. Deterring unplanned absences Unplanned absences can severely impact the bottom line, so preventive measures must be implemented. There must be clear and concise information about the company’s stand on unplanned absences so workers are aware of the disciplinary actions they can face. The best practice at this step is to have a self-service absence management system that allows workers to notify supervisors and managers about absences. 3. Flexible schedule management You can benefit from flexible schedule management, providing workers with enough scope to manage their childcare responsibilities and medical appointments without time off. Effective shift planning is excellent for avoiding tardiness. 4. Incentivize good attendance Rewards are known to reinforce positive feedback. If you have disciplinary measures for tardiness in the attendance policy, you should also include incentives for good attendance. It will encourage workers to avoid unplanned absences. Wage is one of the most significant aspects of employment. Apart from the regular wages, workers also appreciate the opportunity to earn extra income as overtime or supplemental wages. However, for some business owners, supplemental pay is a complex topic. The issue of regular wages vs. supplemental wages can be easily sorted if you understand the definition of both pay types, the misconception about supplemental pay, and how taxation is applied to both salaries. Regular wages vs. supplemental wages: The Definition Regular wages refer to the typical pay workers receive for regular work hours. It is paid to a worker for the standard employment activities and is calculated based on the base salary or hourly rate agreed upon between the worker and employer. The regular pay is subject to regular income tax withholding and other payroll deductions such as Social Security. Supplemental wages are additional payments a worker receives separate from regular wages, such as bonuses, overtime, commissions, severance, and vacation pay. These wages are not part of the workers regular salary or hourly rate and are often one-time or occasional. Furthermore, the supplemental wage is subject to different tax withholding rules. Regular wages vs. supplemental wages: The Taxation Regular wages are subject to federal and state income tax, Social Security tax, and Medicare tax. The federal income tax is calculated on workers regular wages based on their filing status and withholding allowances claimed on their Form W-4 . Also, the amount withheld is based on taxable income and the tax brackets applicable for the tax year. If you are in a state with a state income tax on regular wages, you will be subjected to specific rates and rules depending on your state. Some states have a flat tax rate for regular wages, while others use progressive tax structures like the federal income tax. Social Security tax is also called the Old Age, Survivors, and Disability Insurance (OASDI) tax which is a tax that funds Social Security benefits. The Social Security tax rate in 2023 is 6.2% on wages up to $160,200 annually. Medicare is another federal tax with a flat tax rate of 1.45% on all regular wages. There is no income threshold, and in addition to the workers contribution, the employers are also required to contribute 1.45% of the workers wages to Medicare. Supplemental wages are also subject to federal income tax withholding, but the rules differ from the regular wages tax. There are two methods to calculate supplemental income tax: 1. Aggregate Method: This method is used when an worker is paid regular and supplemental wages during the same payroll period. If a worker earns both wages, the employer can combine the amounts and withhold federal income tax based on the workers total taxable income for that period. The withholding tax rates will depend on the workers withholding status and the number of withholding allowances claimed. 2. Flat Rate Method: If the employers want, they can use a flat supplemental income tax rate to calculate federal income tax. Here, the default rate is 22% for supplemental wages up to $1 million. If the supplemental wages exceed $1 million, the excess amount is taxed at a flat rate of 37%. Employers need to consider the state income tax withholding on supplemental wages as states have rules, which may vary depending on the specific state regulations. Additionally, other local taxes or deductions can be made depending on the jurisdiction. Regular wages vs. supplemental wages: Examples Regular wages include your base salary, which is a fixed agreed-upon salary. It also consists of the hourly wages for contractual staff. Your annual salary is counted as regular wages irrespective of pay periods (e.g., monthly or bi-weekly). For hospitality workers like restaurant servers, the tipped wages are also included in the regular wages as they often work below minimum wage in the expectation that customer tips will make up the difference. Piecework wages are also regular wages, where workers are paid based on the number of units or pieces produced or completed. Allowances for reimbursements of expenses like commuting, uniforms, or job-related costs are also part of regular pay. Any additional pay apart from the regular wages form supplemental wages, including bonuses, overtime pay to commissions, and severance pay. Your vacation pay for unused vacation days or as part of paid time off (PTO) policies is also calculated as supplemental. Non-cash or cash rewards from the employer for exceptional performance or participation in competitions are supplemental wages. Any relocation allowances, such as reimbursements or relocation payments for work purposes, counted as additional income. Any nonqualified deferred compensation, such as payments made tostaff outside of qualified retirement plans or wages paid retroactively to staff for past work due to wage adjustments, corrections, or legal settlements, are also supplemental wages. The examples provided are incomplete, and many more supplemental and regular wages can vary depending on the industry, job roles, and jurisdictions. Regular wages vs. supplemental wages: Reporting requirements There are specific reporting requirements for regular and supplemental wages. For reporting regular wages, employers must provide workers with a Form W-2, Wage, and Tax Statement by January 31st of the following year. This form reports the staff’s wages, tips, other compensation, and taxes withheld throughout the year. The total regular wages are reported in Box 1 of Form W-2. Supplemental wages are also reported on Form W-2. However, they can be reported in either Box 1 (total wages) or Box 3 (Social Security wages) of Form W-2. If some supplemental wages are not reported in Form W-2, they can be reported on Form 1099-MISC as Miscellaneous Income . Importance of distinguishing between supplemental and regular wages Identifying and differentiating between regular and supplemental wages is essential for deciding the appropriate tax withholding methods and rates. As there are separate reporting requirements for regular wages and supplemental wages on tax forms, distinguishing between the two is necessary for accurate tax reporting. For employers, distinguishing between regular and supplemental wages helps to comply with employment laws such as overtime regulations. Finally, differentiating between regular and supplemental wages is essential for a company’s financial planning as it aids in budgeting. Common misconceptions Supplemental wages are taxed higher than regular wages: Supplemental wages are indeed subject to different tax withholding methods and rates, but compared to regular wages, they are not taxed at a higher overall rate. Supplemental income is tax-free: Workers must report any supplemental income to calculate their tax liability. Conclusion For employers, it is necessary to understand the difference between regular wages and supplemental wages.
